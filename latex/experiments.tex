\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \textbf{PCG-MAS} on five research questions (R1--R5) drawn
directly from Sections \ref{sec:theory} and \ref{sec:method}: whether
the Theorem~1 audit-decomposition bound holds empirically (R1),
whether the redundant-consensus law of Theorem~2 produces the predicted
geometric collapse (R2), whether root-cause attribution is recoverable
from an accepted certificate (R3), how the cost-vs-harm Pareto frontier
shifts under the certificate contract (R4), and what token overhead
the framework imposes (R5). Across all five experiments we deliberately
sweep \emph{seven} LLM agents spanning 3.8B to 671B parameters and
\emph{eight} datasets covering reading-comprehension, claim-verification,
biomedical, financial-numeric, web-action, and tool-use regimes.
Cross-cutting diagnostics are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:intro-hero-v4};
the full $7 \times 8$ matrix appears in Appendix \ref{appendix:exp_details}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/intro_hero_v4.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Cross-LLM cross-cutting summary.} Across four LLM
  agents spanning 3.8B--671B parameters (full 7-LLM cohort in
  Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-hero-v3-app}), PCG-MAS reduces harm
  rate by $\sim 20\times$ (median, 95\% paired-bootstrap CIs),
  achieves $\geq 83\%$ Theorem-1 bound tightness (LHS/RHS), and

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp-setup}

\textbf{LLM agents.} Seven agents: \texttt{phi-3.5-mini} (3.8B),
\texttt{qwen2.5-7B}, \texttt{deepseek-llm-7b-chat}, \texttt{Llama-3.1-8B},
\texttt{Gemma-2-9b-it}, \texttt{Llama-3.3-70B}, and \texttt{deepseek-v3}
(671B MoE). Local agents are run via \texttt{transformers} on M-series
hardware; the two largest are run on Colab Pro+ (H100 with 4-bit
quantization for Llama-3.3-70B) and the HF Inference router (DeepSeek-V3).
\textbf{Datasets.} Eight datasets: HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, ToolBench,
FEVER, PubMedQA, TAT-QA, WebLINX, plus a synthetic adversarial split
constructed per Appendix \ref{appendix:exp-synthetic} for stress-testing
the integrity channel. \textbf{Diversity coverage.} Each per-experiment
figure shows three $(\text{LLM}, \text{dataset})$ \emph{cells} drawn from
the $5 \times 8$ locally-runnable matrix under a diversity constraint
(no LLM or dataset repeats within a plot, balanced cross-plot coverage);
the full selection plan and its construction are in Appendix
\ref{appendix:exp-coverage}. \textbf{Reproducibility.} Every figure
caption lists the run identifiers used; the full backend manifest
(\texttt{backend kind}, \texttt{model\_name}, $n_{\text{examples}}$,
seeds) is auto-emitted to Appendix Table~\ref{tab:backends} via
\texttt{\textbackslash input\{artifacts/backends\_manifest.tex\}}.

\subsection{R1: Theorem~1 audit decomposition holds across all cells}
\label{sec:exp-r1}

\textbf{Question.} Does $\Pr[\text{accept} \cap \text{wrong}]
\leq p_{\text{IntFail}} + p_{\text{ReplayFail}} + p_{\text{CheckFail}}
+ p_{\text{CovGap}}$ hold in practice?
\textbf{Setup.} For each cell, we run $n=200$ examples across three seeds,
record empirical accepted-but-wrong probability with 95\% Wilson confidence
intervals, and decompose the right-hand side into the four channel
estimators of Sec.~\ref{sec:theory-audit}.
\textbf{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:r1-audit} shows that the LHS bar sits
strictly below the stacked RHS bar in every cell, with positive slack
$+0.029$ to $+0.052$. Table~\ref{tab:t1-audit} reports the per-channel
contributions and the LHS/RHS ratio. \emph{Theorem~1 is empirically
faithful: the bound is tight (median LHS/RHS $\approx 0.86$) without
ever being violated.}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/r1_audit_decomposition.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{R1.} Per-cell decomposition of $\Pr[\text{accept} \cap
  \text{wrong}]$ (red, LHS) compared to the Theorem~1 right-hand-side
  channel sum (stacked: IntFail, ReplayFail, CheckFail, CovGap).
  Black tick marks at the bar tip show the empirical value with 95\%
  Wilson CI. Across diverse $(\text{LLM}, \text{dataset})$ cells, the
  bound holds with non-trivial slack.}
  \label{fig:r1-audit}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{R2: Redundant consensus collapses geometrically}
\label{sec:exp-r2}

\textbf{Question.} Does the per-claim false-accept probability decay
as $\rho^{k-1}\cdot \varepsilon^k$ when redundancy $k$ grows
(Theorem~2)?
\textbf{Setup.} For each cell, we vary $k \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ and
measure $\Pr[\text{false accept}]$ on a fixed evaluation set with
$\varepsilon_{\text{adv}} \in [0, 0.4]$ swept by injecting adversarial
evidence at controlled rates.
\textbf{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:r2-redundancy} shows the empirical
curve hugging the Theorem~2 bound (dashed) on a log scale; the orange
shaded envelope is the safety regime under the adversary sweep.
The curve is straight in $\log P$ vs.\ $k$ space (the \emph{exact}
prediction of Theorem~2: $\log P = \mathrm{const} + k \cdot
\log(\rho \cdot \varepsilon)$), and lies inside the adversary band
across all 3 cells. Empirical-vs-bound ratios are summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:t2-redundancy}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/r2_redundancy_law.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{R2.} Per-claim false-accept probability vs.\
  redundancy $k$, log-scale. Empirical (red) tracks the Theorem~2
  bound (dashed) closely; the orange band shows the regime envelope
  under adversary fraction $\varepsilon_{\text{adv}} \in [0, 0.4]$.}

\subsection{R3: Responsibility is recoverable from the certificate alone}
\label{sec:exp-r3}

\textbf{Question.} Given an accepted certificate $Z$ that later turns
out to be wrong, can the responsibility-attribution algorithm
(Sec.~\ref{sec:method-responsibility}) identify the failing channel
without re-running the LLM?
\textbf{Setup.} We synthetically inject failures from each of the four
audit regimes (integrity, replay, check, coverage), accept the
certificate, then ask the auditor to recover the regime from $Z$ alone.
\textbf{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:r3-responsibility} shows top-1
accuracy across all four regimes for the three diverse cells; the
mean lift over the no-certificate baseline ($\sim 0.25$ random) is
$+19$ to $+72$ percentage points. Table~\ref{tab:t3-responsibility}
reports per-regime accuracy. Responsibility is recoverable.

\begin{figure*}[t]

  \caption{\textbf{R3.} Top-1 root-cause attribution accuracy by
  regime, comparing the deterministic auditor reading $Z$ alone (red)
  against a no-certificate baseline (navy). Right panel: mean accuracy
  lift in percentage points.}

\end{figure*}

\subsection{R4: PCG-MAS Pareto-dominates baselines on cost vs.\ harm}
\label{sec:exp-r4}

\textbf{Question.} For a fixed compute budget, does the certificate
contract reduce the harm rate compared to alternative answer
policies?
\textbf{Setup.} We compare three policies on the same budget grid:
\textsc{Always-answer} (no abstain), a learned threshold policy, and
PCG-MAS (refuse on certificate failure).
\textbf{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:r4-pareto} shows PCG-MAS
Pareto-dominating both alternatives across all three cells; the per-claim
$(\text{cost}, \text{harm})$ density is overlaid in grey to expose the
dispersion behind the policy curves. Harm reduction factors over the
always-answer baseline are 18--27$\times$. Table~\ref{tab:t4-risk}
tabulates the area-under-Pareto for each policy.

\subsection{R5: The certificate contract costs $\sim 2\times$ tokens}
\label{sec:exp-r5}

\textbf{Question.} What token overhead does PCG-MAS impose, and how
is it distributed across the prove/verify/redundant/audit phases?
\textbf{Setup.} For each cell, we record per-claim token counts for
the no-certificate baseline (single Prover) and for PCG-MAS at the
default $k = 2$ setting; the four phases of $Z$-construction are
budgeted separately.
\textbf{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:r5-overhead} shows token
distributions (top row) and per-phase budgets (bottom row). The
overhead factor is consistently $1.7\text{--}2.3\times$ across cells,
dominated by the redundant-consensus phase. Table~\ref{tab:t5-overhead}
breaks the cost down by phase.

\subsection{Cross-experiment summary}
\label{sec:exp-summary}

Putting R1--R5 together: the Theorem~1 audit-decomposition bound holds
empirically (median LHS/RHS $\approx 0.86$); the Theorem~2 redundancy
collapse is the right functional form on log-scale; responsibility is
recoverable from $Z$ alone with $+19$--$72$\,pp lift; cost-vs-harm is
Pareto-dominated by PCG-MAS; and the price tag is $\sim 2 \times$
tokens. The condensed 2$\times$3 cross-experiment KPI grid is
Figure~\ref{fig:summary} in Appendix~\ref{appendix:exp_details}.
